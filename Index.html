Unit I: Basic Concepts of Physics

Welcome to Unit I of Radiation Physics! This unit lays the groundwork for understanding how radiation interacts with matter, which is fundamental to all medical imaging modalities. We will explore the very essence of matter, delving into the structure of atoms and the forces that govern their behavior, culminating in an introduction to the different types of radiation encountered in medical settings.

1. Atomic Structure

Atoms are the fundamental building blocks of all matter. Understanding their composition and structure is crucial to comprehending X-ray production and how radiation interacts with biological tissues.

  • Subatomic Particles: Atoms consist of a central nucleus surrounded by orbiting electrons.
    • Protons: Positively charged particles ($+1e$) found in the nucleus. Their number (Atomic Number, Z) defines the element. Mass is approximately 1 atomic mass unit (amu).
    • Neutrons: Electrically neutral particles found in the nucleus. They contribute to the atom's mass but not its charge. Mass is approximately 1 amu.
    • Electrons: Negatively charged particles ($-1e$) orbiting the nucleus. They have negligible mass compared to protons and neutrons. In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons.
  • Isotopes, Isobars, Isotones: These terms classify atomic nuclei based on their proton and neutron counts.
    • Isotopes: Atoms of the same element (same Z) but with different numbers of neutrons (different A). E.g., $^{12}\text{C}$ and $^{14}\text{C}$.
    • Isobars: Atoms with the same mass number (same A) but different atomic numbers (different Z). E.g., $^{40}\text{Ar}$ (Z=18) and $^{40}\text{K}$ (Z=19).
    • Isotones: Atoms with the same number of neutrons (same N) but different atomic numbers (different Z) and mass numbers (different A). E.g., $^{36}\text{S}$ (N=20) and $^{37}\text{Cl}$ (N=20).
  • Bohr’s Atomic Model & Energy Levels: This model describes electrons orbiting the nucleus in discrete, stable energy levels or shells.
    • Electrons exist in specific, quantized energy shells, denoted by principal quantum numbers ($n=1, 2, 3, \ldots$) or by letters (K, L, M, ...). K-shell corresponds to $n=1$, L-shell to $n=2$, and so on.
    • Electrons in shells closer to the nucleus (e.g., K-shell) are more tightly bound and require more energy to remove. This is their electron binding energy.
    • Electrons can only jump between these discrete energy levels by absorbing or emitting a photon with an energy exactly equal to the energy difference between the shells. This principle is fundamental to Characteristic X-ray production, where an outer-shell electron fills an inner-shell vacancy, releasing an X-ray photon.

2. Mass Defect and Binding Energy

Beyond electron binding, the nucleus itself is held together by immense forces. The energy associated with these forces is described by mass defect and nuclear binding energy.

  • Mass Defect: The actual mass of a stable nucleus is always slightly less than the sum of the masses of its individual constituent protons and neutrons (nucleons) if they were free. This 'missing' mass is called the mass defect ($ \Delta m $).
  • Derivation using E=mc²: This mass defect is directly converted into the energy that binds the nucleus together, known as the nuclear binding energy ($ E_B $), according to Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle: $ E_B = \Delta m \cdot c^2 $, where $c$ is the speed of light.
    • A larger binding energy per nucleon indicates greater nuclear stability.
  • Nuclear Stability: Nuclei strive for stability. The binding energy per nucleon curve shows that intermediate-mass nuclei (around Iron-56) are the most stable.
    • Lighter nuclei can undergo fusion (combining) to become more stable and release energy.
    • Heavier nuclei can undergo fission (splitting) to become more stable and release energy.
  • Packing Fraction: Defined as the mass defect per nucleon (($A_{mass} - A_{number}) / A_{number}$). It provides another measure of nuclear stability, where negative values indicate greater stability.

3. Energy and Its Units

In radiation physics, energy is often expressed in specific units to simplify calculations and conceptualization.

  • Joule (J): The SI unit of energy. Defined as the work done when a force of one Newton acts over a distance of one meter. ($1 \text{ J} = 1 \text{ N} \cdot \text{m} = 1 \text{ kg} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2$).
  • Erg (erg): The CGS unit of energy. Defined as the work done by a force of one dyne acting over one centimeter. ($1 \text{ erg} = 1 \text{ dyne} \cdot \text{cm} = 1 \text{ g} \cdot \text{cm}^2/\text{s}^2$).
    • Conversion: $1 \text{ J} = 10^7 \text{ erg}$.
  • Electron Volt (eV): The energy gained by an electron when it moves through an electric potential difference of one volt. This unit is particularly useful in atomic and nuclear physics due to the small energies involved.
    • Conversion: $1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$.
    • Common multiples: kilo-electron volt (keV) = $10^3 \text{ eV}$, Mega-electron volt (MeV) = $10^6 \text{ eV}$.
    • Medical Relevance: Diagnostic X-rays typically range from 10 keV to 150 keV. Gamma rays used in nuclear medicine, such as those from Technetium-99m, have an energy of 141 keV, while PET annihilation photons are 511 keV.

4. Introduction to Radiation

Radiation is energy emitted in the form of waves or particles. In medical imaging, we primarily deal with ionizing radiation, which has enough energy to remove electrons from atoms.

  • Types of Ionizing Radiation:
    • Alpha (α) Particles: Consist of two protons and two neutrons (a Helium nucleus). Positively charged ($+2e$). High mass, low penetration (stopped by paper), but high Linear Energy Transfer (LET) if inside the body. Not used for imaging; hazardous if ingested. Origin: Alpha decay of heavy nuclei.
    • Beta (β) Particles: Energetic electrons (β⁻) or positrons (β⁺). Low mass, moderate penetration (stopped by a few mm of aluminum). Beta-plus decay is crucial for PET imaging. Origin: Beta decay of unstable nuclei.
    • Gamma (γ) Rays: High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons) emitted from the nucleus during radioactive decay (e.g., after alpha or beta decay). No mass, no charge, high penetration (requires thick lead/concrete), low LET. Used in Nuclear Medicine for imaging. Origin: Nuclear transitions.
    • X-rays: High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons) produced outside the nucleus, typically from the interaction of fast electrons with a target material. No mass, no charge, high penetration. Widely used in Diagnostic Radiology (e.g., radiography, CT). Origin: Electron interactions (Bremsstrahlung, Characteristic X-rays).
  • Natural vs. Artificial Radiation Sources:
    • Natural Sources: Contribute about 50% of an average person's annual radiation dose. Examples include:
      • Cosmic radiation (from outer space, higher at altitude).
      • Terrestrial radiation (from radioactive elements in the Earth's crust, e.g., Uranium, Thorium, Radon gas).
      • Internal radiation (from naturally occurring radionuclides within the body, e.g., Potassium-40).
    • Artificial Sources: Contribute about 50% of an average person's annual radiation dose, with the vast majority coming from medical procedures. Examples include:
      • Medical Diagnostic Procedures (X-rays, CT scans, Nuclear Medicine scans).
      • Medical Therapeutic Procedures (Radiation therapy).
      • Consumer products (e.g., old luminous watches, some smoke detectors).
      • Industrial uses, research, nuclear power generation (minor contribution to public dose).
